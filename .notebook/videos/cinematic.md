Create a cinematic video overview promoting SYSEN 5470: Network 
Science and Applications for Systems Engineering at Cornell, taught 
by Tim Fraser. Target length: 60-90 seconds. Audience: prospective 
graduate students browsing the course catalog or considering enrolling.

PURPOSE
Make a thinking engineer want to take this course. Not by selling — 
by showing them a class of problems they didn't know had a name.

OPENING (10-15 sec) — THE QUIET HOOK
Open with a single engineering failure. Not framed as failure — 
framed as a question.
"A storm hits Florida. Cell towers go down. Who can still talk to 
whom — and which connections, if you restored them first, would 
reconnect the most people?"
No course title yet. Let the question sit.

MIDDLE (40-60 sec) — WHAT THE COURSE DOES
Build three short vignettes, each ~15 sec, each anchored in a 
different engineered system. Use case studies from the course:
- A transit network: which station, if it fails, breaks the most 
  trips?
- A supply chain: which supplier failure cascades the furthest?
- A disaster evacuation: which route should you reinforce first?

Each vignette: one engineering question, one network drawing 
emerging on screen, one moment of insight (mustard yellow highlight 
on the answer).

The narration through this section names what the course teaches — 
but as verbs, not topics: "You'll learn to see systems as networks. 
To measure what matters. To predict what breaks first."

CLOSE (10-15 sec) — THE INVITATION
Course title appears: SYSEN 5470 · Network Science and Applications 
for Systems Engineering · Cornell.
One line of subhead: "Three weeks. Real systems. Network thinking."
Tim's name and contact, briefly.
Final visual: a sketchpad with one drawn network, marker still on 
the page.

VISUAL STYLE
Cinematic but understated. Cool color palette — deep navy, cream, 
mustard yellow accent. Networks are the recurring visual motif — 
they emerge, evolve, light up. Not glossy 3D. Not corporate stock. 
Closer to a thoughtful documentary than a tech ad.
Sketchpad moments where networks are drawn by hand, blending into 
the cinematic frame.

TONE
Confident, calm, a little dry. Like a senior engineer describing 
work they care about. No hype words. No exclamation marks. No 
"unleash" or "unlock" or "revolutionary".

PACING
Slower than a typical promo. Let visuals breathe. Trust the viewer 
to read a network diagram.

NO
- No talking heads
- No stock footage of generic "data" or "technology"  
- No hand-on-keyboard typing shots
- No "Are you ready to..." or "Discover..." openers
- No music swell at the title reveal

GROUNDING
Pull case study scenarios from notebook sources. Use real course 
content as the backbone of the three vignettes.
